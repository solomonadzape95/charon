Onboarding flow (3 screens, not a page)
After email signup, three screens before they hit the main app:
Screen 1 — "How Charon works." Three sentences max. You read. We track engagement. We pay the creator automatically after your session. One illustration showing the flow.
Screen 2 — Deposit. Card input via Circle's embedded onramp widget. Suggested amounts: $3 / $5 / $10 with a custom field. Show what each gets them in approximate chapters: "$5 ≈ 100-250 chapters depending on what you read." First-time users also see the $0.50 free credit applied automatically here — "We're adding $0.50 to your balance to get you started."
Screen 3 — Pick your first series. Show 6-8 featured series, genre-filtered by what they indicated on signup. Tap one, go directly to the series page. No extra steps.

Home / Discovery page
The reader's main feed after login. Not a social feed — a reading-focused dashboard.
Top section — Continue reading. Cards for every series they've started, showing their current chapter and the number of unread chapters since they last visited. If they're a pre-release subscriber to a series and a new chapter just dropped, that card is highlighted with "New chapter available."
Middle section — Trending this week. Series where reader engagement is highest — not just views, but session depth and completion rates. Agent 3's momentum scores determine this ranking, not manual curation. This is important: the ranking reflects genuine reader engagement, not paid placement.
Bottom section — Recommended for you. Agent 4 powers this — based on genres they've read, binge patterns, and completion rates, it surfaces series they're likely to love. This is a stretch goal for the hackathon but include the UI placeholder even if the logic is simple at first.
What the agent touches: Agent 3 sets the trending rankings. Agent 4 powers recommendations. Neither blocks page load — both are pre-computed on a schedule and cached.

Series page
The page for a specific series. This is where a reader decides whether to start reading.
What's on it:

Cover image, title, author, genre tags
Series description
Stats: total chapters, total readers, average session value (shows creators what the community thinks it's worth), update frequency ("new chapter every 3-4 days")
Payment mode selector — three options shown clearly:

Read as you go (pay per session, agent-valued)
Series Pass (one price, permanent access including future chapters — shown with Agent 2's suggested price)
Pre-release (auto-pay when new chapters drop, shown with per-chapter price)


Chapter list — each chapter shows current price (Agent 3), word count, estimated read time, and a small engagement indicator (completion rate visualized as a simple bar — high completion chapters are visually distinct)
Reader comments section — kept minimal, no threading, just reactions to chapters
"Start reading" button — goes directly to chapter 1

What the agent touches: Agent 3's current price on every chapter. Agent 2's Series Pass suggested price. Completion rate data fed by Agent 1's historical session data across all readers.

Chapter reader page
The most important page on the entire platform. This is where value is created and where the UX has to be perfect.
What's on it:

The content. Nothing else while reading.
Minimal chrome: series title, chapter number, a thin progress bar at the top, a settings icon (font size, background color — white/sepia/dark)
For manga/manhwa: vertical scroll by default, tap-to-zoom on panels
For webnovel: clean typography, comfortable line height, no distractions
No payment UI. No price display. No balance reminder. Nothing that reminds the reader money is involved while they're reading.
At the very bottom after the last line: chapter navigation (previous / next), a simple reaction strip (five emoji reactions — quick, no account friction), and a comment field (optional, one line)

What the agent touches: Agent 1 starts a silent session timer the moment the page loads. It tracks scroll events, direction changes (scroll-backs), time between scroll events (reading pace vs skimming), and completion. None of this is visible to the reader. None of it creates any UI. It is purely background instrumentation.

Session summary (overlay / screen after navigating away)
This is the most novel UI element on the platform. It appears either as a bottom sheet when the reader navigates to the next chapter, or as a full screen when they close the app or browser tab.
What's on it:

What you read this session: series name, chapters covered, total read time
Session value: the USDC amount Agent 1 settled, shown large and clean — "$0.23"
Agent reasoning: one sentence explaining why. "You re-read chapter 7 twice and binged 5 chapters without pausing. High engagement session." This is the moment that makes the platform feel intelligent rather than mechanical.
Balance remaining: shown small, below the main number
Creator acknowledgment: "Settled to [Creator Name]" with their avatar. Humanizes the transaction.
Two buttons: "Keep reading" and "Done"

If the reader's balance is getting low (Agent 4 has flagged this), a soft nudge appears below the balance: "You have about 12 chapters left at your reading pace. Top up?" — with a one-tap deposit button.
What the agent touches: This entire screen is Agent 1's output. The value, the reasoning, the engagement signals — all from the session analysis. Agent 4 determines whether the low-balance nudge appears.

Reader dashboard
The reader's personal account page. Not prominent — they shouldn't need to visit it often — but complete.
What's on it:

Current balance with top-up button
Reading stats: total chapters read, total creators supported, total USDC settled this month, reading streak (days in a row)
Series following list with mode shown per series (pay-as-you-go / pre-release / series pass)
Full tip history: every session, date, series, chapters read, amount settled, agent reasoning
Spending breakdown: by creator, by genre, by week — simple chart
Payment mode management: toggle any series between modes, cancel pre-release subscriptions
Account settings: email, notification preferences, connected wallet (optional)

What the agent touches: Agent 4 has a dedicated card here showing "Your reading pace this week" and any suggestions — mode switches, top-up recommendations. It's informational, not intrusive.

Creator Side
Creator landing page (unauthenticated)
Separate from the reader landing page. Creators who find Charon through community posts or direct outreach land here.
What's on it:

The creator pitch — one paragraph, no jargon: "Upload your series. Readers pay per chapter based on how deeply they engage. You earn in real time. Withdraw to your bank or wallet with no minimum threshold."
The fee table — explicit, visible, no hiding: "We take 5%. You keep 95%. No other fees."
The global payout proof point — "Instant USDC settlement to any wallet worldwide. No minimums. No conversion delays." This line exists specifically for creators in Nigeria, Indonesia, Philippines, Brazil — every market where Patreon and WebNovel have failed them.
A live earnings ticker if possible — showing real creator earnings on the platform in real time
Comparison table: Charon vs Patreon vs WebNovel vs Royal Road+Patreon on three axes: revenue share, payout speed, reading experience

Creator onboarding (4 screens)
Screen 1 — Basic profile. Name, bio, profile image, genre tags.
Screen 2 — Payout setup. Two options clearly presented:

USDC wallet address (paste it in, done, instant withdrawals)
Bank account (country, account details, Circle handles the offramp — shown with the 1.5% conversion fee disclosed upfront)

This screen is mandatory before upload. Creators need to be able to receive payment before their content goes live.

Screen 3 — Upload your first series. Title, description, genre, cover image. Then chapter upload — text paste or file upload (.txt, .docx, .epub for novels; image upload for manga). Agent 2 runs automatically after upload and shows the suggested price in a confirmation step: "Agent suggests $0.04 per chapter based on word count and genre benchmarks. Accept or set your own price."
Screen 4 — Series Pass setup (optional). Creator can set a Series Pass price or let Agent 2 suggest one. Can be skipped and configured later.

Creator dashboard (home)
The main page a creator sees every time they log in. Should feel motivating — they should see money moving.
What's on it:

Earnings overview — big, prominent. Total earned today, this week, this month. A simple line chart showing earnings over time. This is the first thing they see.
Live activity feed — real-time: "A reader just finished chapter 12 of your series. $0.04 settled." Not overwhelming — capped at showing the last 5 events, auto-updating. This is the emotional core of the creator experience. Seeing money arrive in real time, connected to a specific reader finishing a specific chapter, is the thing that makes creators evangelical about the platform.
Series cards — each series showing: active readers this week, total chapters, earnings this month, average completion rate, and a quick "upload chapter" button
Withdrawal button — always visible, shows available balance. If the 7-day escrow model is active, shows two numbers: "Available now: $3.20 / Pending release: $1.45 (releases in 3 days)"
Creator Health Score (post-hackathon) — shown as a simple gauge. Tells the creator where they stand and what's driving their score.

What the agent touches: Agent 2's pricing recommendations surface here when new chapters have been uploaded. Agent 3's repricing decisions are logged here — creators can see when a chapter's price went up or down and why.

Series management page
One page per series, accessed from the dashboard.
What's on it:

Series details editor — cover, description, genre tags, status (ongoing / completed / hiatus)
Chapter list — every chapter with:

Current price (Agent 3's current valuation, with a small indicator if it's moved up or down recently)
Read count
Completion rate (shown as a percentage bar — instantly shows which chapters readers finish and which they abandon)
Re-read rate
Earnings from this chapter
Edit / delete options


Chapter upload button — opens a simple upload flow inline
Pricing controls — override Agent 2/3 prices per chapter, or set a global price floor for the series
Series Pass management — current price, how many readers have bought it, total earned from passes
Pre-release management — set early-access price per chapter, see how many pre-release subscribers you have
Reader retention chart — a simple chart showing what percentage of readers who started the series are still reading at each chapter. The drop-off points are visible. This is genuinely useful data creators have never had before.

What the agent touches: Agent 3's price column is live — it updates as the repricing agent runs. Completion rate and re-read rate data come from Agent 1's accumulated session records across all readers.

Analytics page
Deeper data for creators who want it. Kept simple — not a data science dashboard, just the numbers that actually help a creator make decisions.
What's on it:

Earnings breakdown — by chapter, by time period, by payment mode (how much came from pay-as-you-go vs pre-release vs series pass)
Reader breakdown — total unique readers, new vs returning this week, geographic distribution (anonymized — shows country, not identity)
Engagement heatmap — across all chapters, shows completion rate and re-read rate as a visual heat map. Cold chapters are instantly visible. This tells creators which chapters are weak before readers tell them in comments.
Binge depth chart — average number of chapters read per session. If this is going up, the series has momentum. If it's dropping, readers are losing interest.
Top earning chapters — the 5 chapters that have generated the most revenue. Usually correlates with highest completion and re-read rates.
Payout history — every withdrawal, date, amount, destination, status

What the agent touches: All of this data is derived from Agent 1's session records. The heatmap is the most powerful thing on this page — it visualizes thousands of individual agent valuations into a single actionable picture.
